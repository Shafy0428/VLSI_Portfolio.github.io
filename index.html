About Me

My interest in VLSI and digital electronics began through coursework in digital logic design and computer architecture, where I first encountered the relationship between hardware description languages and actual silicon behaviour. Since then, I have been pursuing a deeper understanding of how digital circuits are specified, verified, implemented, and eventually realised as integrated circuits.

I am particularly drawn to RTL design because it sits at the intersection of engineering rigour and creative problem solving. Writing RTL that is clean, synthesisable, and timing-aware requires both technical precision and careful architectural thinking — qualities I am actively developing through academic projects and self-directed study.

Functional verification is another area I find compelling. Ensuring that a design behaves correctly before it is committed to silicon is one of the most important and intellectually demanding aspects of modern chip development. I am learning testbench development and simulation workflows to contribute meaningfully to verification efforts.

FPGA platforms give me the opportunity to validate digital designs on real hardware, bridging the gap between simulation and physical implementation. I am studying synthesis workflows, constraint-based timing closure, and hardware validation using FPGA development boards.

My long-term goal is to contribute to semiconductor design teams — either in digital design, verification, or physical design — and to work on circuits that operate at the boundary of what current CMOS technology allows.

VLSI Design Flow

Simplified ASIC design flow for reference only. This diagram illustrates general industry stages. It does not imply that I have completed every stage listed, nor that I have taped out a chip.

  1. 1 Specification
    Define functionality, interfaces, and performance targets.
  2. 2 Architecture
    Partition the design, define micro-architecture and block diagrams.
  3. 3 RTL Design
    Describe hardware behaviour in Verilog / SystemVerilog.
  4. 4 Functional Verification
    Simulate and verify design correctness with testbenches.
  5. 5 Logic Synthesis
    Map RTL to a gate-level netlist targeting a specific library.
  6. 6 Design for Test
    Insert scan chains and BIST structures to enable post-silicon testing.
  7. 7 Floorplanning
    Arrange macro blocks and define power grid structure.
  8. 8 Placement
    Place standard cells to minimise wirelength and meet timing.
  9. 9 Clock Tree Synthesis
    Build a balanced clock distribution network to minimise skew.
  10. 10 Routing
    Connect placed cells with metal wires following design rules.
  11. 11 Static Timing Analysis
    Verify that all timing paths meet setup and hold constraints.
  12. 12 Physical Verification
    Run DRC and LVS checks to confirm layout correctness.
  13. 13 Tapeout
    Send finalised GDSII to the fabrication facility.
